# On the Mutual Implication of Laws and Phenomena
## A Note on What Relational Actualism Reveals About Nature
### Joshua F. Sandeman | April 2026

---

Physics has always assumed a hierarchy: laws on one side, phenomena on the other. The laws — conservation of energy, the Einstein field equations, the Born rule — are the fixed background structure against which phenomena play out. They are what governs; phenomena are what gets governed. This division feels natural, almost definitional. What else would a law of nature be, if not something that nature obeys?

Relational Actualism dissolves this dichotomy — not by arguing against it philosophically, but by making it mathematically untenable. And it does so while fully preserving what makes phenomena real: the linear, irreversible unfolding of actualization events in time. RA does not flatten Nature into a timeless web of implications. It shows that the arrow of time and the web of mutual implications are two perspectives on one process.

The framework has a single ontological commitment: that actualization events are primitive. From this, everything else follows. But "follows" is the wrong word, because it implies a derivation sequence — first the axiom, then the theorems, step by step in order. What actually happens is more like simultaneous implication. The LLC is not a constraint that the causal graph satisfies; it *is* the causal graph, stated as a property. Conservation of energy is not a law that actualization events obey; it is what actualization events *are*, viewed at the level of bookkeeping. The irreversibility of time is not explained by the framework; it is encoded in the framework's most primitive commitment, since actualization — by definition — is directed.

The evidence for this is structural. When we worked through the open problems in the programme — nine precisely scoped gaps — and asked which already-proved results each one coupled to, none of them required a genuinely new physical input. Each was an instance of something already in the framework, viewed from a different angle. O09 (do spacelike stochastic updates commute?) was amplitude locality (O01) applied to two observers instead of one. O05 (does an Unruh detector click?) was rindler_stationarity (L05) applied to a measuring device instead of a thermal bath. O08 (what is the biological decoherence timescale?) revealed a fixed-point: μ = ΔS* for any single molecular interaction at any length scale, because the ℓ, Γ, and τ_d all cancel. The same threshold that governs quantum measurement (ΔS* ≈ 0.601 nats, derived from the BDG integers) determines when chemistry crosses into life.

This is not a coincidence of parameters. It is the framework being what it claims to be: one nature of Nature, from which all phenomena arise.

The coupling analysis only found couplings because it asked the right question: not "how do we prove this?" but "what is this *an instance of*?" The answer, every time, was something we already had. The open problems were not independent constraints that the theory happened to satisfy. They were the *same constraint* — "when does a process become irreversible?" — asked at the quantum, cosmological, gravitational, and biological scales simultaneously. The answer is always ΔS*, because that question has only one answer when irreversibility is the primitive.

This is not the same as saying that Nature is static or that time is illusory. Quite the opposite. Actualization events happen in a definite causal order. The graph grows in one direction only. Earlier vertices are permanent — they constitute the factual past. The arrow of time in RA is not a thermodynamic approximation or a feature of our coarse-grained descriptions; it is written into the framework's most primitive commitment, at the level of individual events. The unfolding is real.

What RA shows is that this irreversible unfolding and the web of mutual implications that makes it coherent are not two separate things that need to be reconciled. A theorem and its proof are not the same object, but neither is the theorem "above" the proof — the theorem is what the proof *means*. In RA, the laws are what the actualization events *mean* at the level of pattern. The events unfold in time; the pattern is timeless in the sense that it could not have been otherwise. But the pattern does not precede the events — it emerges from them, and is instantiated by them, and could not exist without them.

---

This raises a presentation problem that is also a conceptual problem. Human language is linear. A paper has a first sentence and a last sentence. An argument has premises and a conclusion. But the structure of RA is not a chain — it is a web. Every node in the dependency graph has multiple parents and multiple children. The Lean-verified Local Ledger Condition (L01) sits at the center of the gravitational theory, the black hole information argument, the WIMP prohibition, and the Bianchi identity. The BDG Poisson-CSG acceptance probability (C02) sits at the center of quantum measurement timescales, the five-scale μ=1 unification, the Bekenstein-Hawking area law, and the minimum unit of biological complexity. These are not separate results that happen to share a number. They are the same result, expressed in different coordinates.

The frustration of presenting RA is the frustration of being asked to read a network as a sequence. Any linear path through it misses the structure that gives it meaning. The theory is correct not because each claim follows from the previous one, but because the entire web is self-consistent — which is a different kind of correctness, and one that requires a different kind of presentation.

The interactive dependency graph that accompanies this note is an attempt at that presentation. It is not a summary or a diagram of the theory. It *is* the theory's logical structure, made visible. The reader can navigate it in any order, following edges in either direction, and the structure will be the same regardless of where they start — because the structure is not imposed on the content, but *is* the content.

The web is the theory. The theory is the web.

---

*What RA ultimately reveals is that the dichotomy between "laws" and "phenomena" is a feature of how we describe Nature, not a feature of Nature itself. The laws are not above the phenomena; they are the phenomena, described at the level of pattern rather than instance. And the deepest patterns — irreversibility, conservation, the direction of time — are not imposed on actualization events from outside. They are what actualization events are.*

*The linear unfolding of events in time is real — more real in RA than in any block-universe framework, since the growing graph cannot run backwards. And the web of mutual implications that makes that unfolding coherent is equally real. These are not two competing descriptions. They are two aspects of one ontology: Nature unfolding in time, in the only way that is self-consistent.*
