
# RA Framing Discipline: Native First, Comparative Always, Derivative Never

**Date:** April 23, 2026  
**Status:** Core framing principle for the RA suite. Replaces prior framing documents where they create ambiguity.

## The central rule

Relational Actualism (RA) must be presented **natively first** and **comparatively second**.

That means:

1. **RA's proof path runs through its own primitives**: DAG growth, the BDG acceptance kernel, the Local Ledger Condition (LLC), finite actualization, motif renewal, severance, and whatever additional structures are explicitly introduced within the RA framework.
2. **Existing theories must be used explicitly for comparison and contrast** whenever doing so helps the reader understand:
   - what empirical domain RA is addressing,
   - how RA differs in mechanism,
   - where RA has no analogue of a familiar construct,
   - what is already open versus what RA already explains.
3. **Existing theories are not RA's derivational scaffold.** They are not the proof path, not the source of RA's primitive objects, and not the authority by which RA is validated.

This means the suite should not be written as though quantum mechanics, the Standard Model, general relativity, or other established theories are irrelevant. They are highly relevant as the current scientific organizations of empirical domains RA also seeks to address. But RA is not to be derived *through* them.

## The right editorial stance

The correct editorial stance is:

> **Use comparison aggressively for orientation, stakes, and contrast; do not use existing theory as RA's mechanism or proof path.**

That is the suite doctrine.

## What is allowed and encouraged

The following are not only allowed, but often desirable.

### 1. Immediate lineage statements
Examples:
- “RA is in the immediate mathematical lineage of causal set theory.”
- “Like causal set theory, RA begins from a locally finite causal order.”
- “RA adds primitive irreversible actualization, the realized/potentia split, the LLC, and local use of the d=4 BDG score.”

These statements help the reader place RA.

### 2. Domain-orientation statements
Examples:
- “The Standard Model organizes the empirical domain of particle physics.”
- “General relativity organizes the empirical domain of gravitational phenomenology.”
- “Quantum mechanics organizes amplitudes, frequencies, and measurement statistics.”

These statements are acceptable when they orient the reader to the domain RA is entering.

### 3. Mechanism-contrast statements
Examples:
- “RA addresses the same observational domain by a different mechanism.”
- “RA does not begin from gauge groups or running couplings as primitive objects.”
- “RA does not begin from a metric field on a differentiable manifold.”
- “RA does not take a Born rule or collapse postulate as primitive.”

These are often essential.

### 4. “No analogue” statements
Examples:
- “RA has no direct analogue of the standard-theory construct.”
- “Causal severance has no close analogue in standard QM or GR.”
- “RA does not contain a primitive equivalent of the Born rule.”

These statements are scientifically valuable because they tell the reader that RA is not merely renaming familiar machinery.

### 5. Comparative cartography
It is legitimate to say that a familiar theory:
- organizes the same observational domain,
- gives a different mechanism,
- provides a useful comparison class,
- reaches similar observables by a different mathematical route.

This is part of good exposition.

## What must be avoided

The following framings are not acceptable.

### 1. Existing theory as proof path
Avoid wording that implies:
- RA is derived through QM / QFT / SM / GR,
- RA is proved by borrowing the mechanism of those theories,
- RA needs those theories in order to state its own primitives.

### 2. Existing theory as benchmark in the strong sense
Avoid wording that implies:
- RA succeeds only if it reproduces the formal objects of existing theories one by one,
- RA owes a native counterpart to every standard-theory category,
- agreement with Standard Model or GR formalism is the primary criterion of success.

Agreement with measured nature is primary. Agreement with legacy formalism is secondary and often optional.

### 3. Imported ontology as RA ontology
Avoid silently importing as primitive:
- gauge groups,
- quantum numbers,
- renormalization machinery,
- field content,
- vacuum-energy ontology,
- continuum metric ontology,
unless the paper is explicitly discussing these as comparative language rather than native RA structure.

### 4. False equivalence language
Avoid phrases like:
- “RA is just another route to...”
- “RA's version of X” when X is a standard-theory construct that RA does not actually possess,
- “RA recovers Y” when what is really meant is “RA addresses the same observational domain” or “RA offers a possible downstream translation.”

## The test for any passage

For any explanatory sentence, ask:

1. **Is the mechanism stated in RA-native terms?**  
   If no, the sentence likely needs revision.

2. **Is the comparison being used for reader orientation rather than proof support?**  
   If yes, it is usually acceptable.

3. **Does the sentence falsely imply that RA owes a counterpart to every construct in existing theory?**  
   If yes, revise it.

4. **Does the sentence clarify where RA differs or has no analogue?**  
   If yes, it is probably doing useful work.

## Standard sentence patterns to prefer

These are encouraged.

### Good pattern A
“RA addresses the same empirical domain as [existing theory], but by a different mechanism.”

### Good pattern B
“[Existing theory] provides the reader's current organization of the observational domain; RA does not borrow its mechanism.”

### Good pattern C
“RA has no direct analogue of [standard construct]; this is a structural difference, not a deficiency in exposition.”

### Good pattern D
“The comparison is explanatory cartography, not active proof support.”

### Good pattern E
“RA may ultimately agree with [existing theory] on some observables while differing sharply in mechanism.”

## Standard sentence patterns to avoid

These are discouraged.

### Bad pattern A
“RA derives X by translating Y from the Standard Model / GR / QM.”

### Bad pattern B
“RA must recover every major construct of current theory.”

### Bad pattern C
“RA is validated because it looks like [existing theory].”

### Bad pattern D
“RA’s target is [theory] rather than the observed world.”

## Final commitment

RA should be introduced as a genuinely new theory, not as a disguised rephrasing of existing theory.  
But a genuinely new theory must be made intelligible to readers, and intelligibility requires comparison.

Therefore:

> **RA is stated natively, situated comparatively, and never derived by borrowing the ontology or mechanism of the theories it contrasts with.**
