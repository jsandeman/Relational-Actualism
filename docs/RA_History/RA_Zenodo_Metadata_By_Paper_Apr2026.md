## Parent Record — Relational Actualism: Four-Paper Preprint Suite

### Title
Relational Actualism: A Four-Paper Preprint Suite on Discrete Causal Physics

### Resource type
Publication → Preprint, or Dataset/Other if uploading only the ZIP bundle. Prefer Publication → Preprint if Zenodo permits the suite bundle as a publication.

### Description
Relational Actualism is a discrete framework for fundamental physics in which physical reality is modeled as a finite, evolving causal graph. The theory builds on causal set theory but introduces additional structure: irreversible actualization as a primitive process, a local conservation law at each event, and a finite growth rule based on the Benincasa–Dowker–Glaser (BDG) action. In this framework, spacetime, matter, and large-scale organization are treated as emergent properties of causal network dynamics rather than as primitive continuum entities.

The suite develops this framework across four domains. Paper I establishes the foundational kernel: the axioms, growth rule, and discrete event-formation process. Paper II develops a matter programme in which stable motifs in the causal graph correspond to persistent physical structures and interactions. Paper III develops a gravitational and cosmological programme based on finite causal structure, including causal severance as an alternative to singular continuation. Paper IV extends the framework to complexity, life, and cognition, proposing structural conditions for recursive closure and persistent organization.

Across the suite, the theory is developed from its own primitives while explicitly comparing its claims and mechanisms with existing physical and scientific frameworks. The aim is not to reproduce quantum mechanics, the Standard Model, or general relativity term-for-term, but to address the same empirical domains through a distinct discrete mechanism grounded in causal structure.

relational actualism; causal set theory; discrete spacetime; quantum gravity; causal networks; emergence; complex systems

## Title
Relational Actualism I: Discrete Spacetime, Actualization, and the BDG Growth Kernel

## Optional subtitle
A causal-set-based framework for finite sequential growth and event formation

## Resource type
Publication → Preprint

## Description
Relational Actualism I introduces a discrete framework for fundamental physics in which spacetime is modeled as a finite causal graph evolving through irreversible event formation.

Building on causal set theory, the paper defines a sequential growth process governed by the Benincasa–Dowker–Glaser (BDG) action and a local conservation law, the Local Ledger Condition. Unlike standard formulations of quantum theory and relativity, the framework treats actualization itself as primitive and does not assume a continuum spacetime, wavefunction, or collapse rule.

The paper establishes the core kernel of the theory: its axioms, growth rule, acceptance criterion, and active support surface. It also identifies open targets including quantum-statistical frequency laws, covariance closure, and macroscopic spacetime structure.

# Paper II

## Title
Relational Actualism II: Emergent Matter and Interaction from Discrete Motif Structure

## Optional subtitle
A causal-network approach to particle phenomenology beyond field-theoretic primitives

## Resource type
Publication → Preprint

## Description
Relational Actualism II develops a discrete, causal-network-based approach to matter and interaction.

Instead of treating particles and forces as fundamental fields, the framework models matter as a finite set of stable motifs arising from local growth dynamics in a causal graph. Interaction ranges, confinement, and structural asymmetries are interpreted as consequences of closure, renewal, and conservation constraints rather than gauge symmetry or field dynamics.

The paper addresses the same empirical domain as particle physics, including masses, lifetimes, interaction ranges, and matter-sector asymmetries, while proposing a distinct underlying mechanism based on discrete causal structure. Where quantitative closure is not yet achieved, including scattering predictions and full mapping to observed particle spectra, the paper states explicit native routes forward within the motif-based framework.

# Paper III

## Title
Relational Actualism III: Discrete Gravity, Cosmology, and Causal Severance

## Optional subtitle
A finite causal-graph approach to horizons, entropy, and large-scale structure

## Resource type
Publication → Preprint

## Description
Relational Actualism III develops a discrete approach to gravity and cosmology based on finite causal structure.

The framework replaces smooth spacetime geometry as a primitive with a dynamically evolving causal graph and introduces causal severance: a finite graph-partition mechanism triggered by saturation of the event-formation kernel. Causal severance is proposed as an alternative to singular continuation and has no close analogue in standard gravitational frameworks.

Black-hole entropy, horizon structure, and large-scale cosmological phenomena are interpreted in terms of boundary counts, sparse-regime topology, and inherited causal structure rather than metric curvature or vacuum energy as primitive inputs. The paper outlines a programme for explaining gravitational and cosmological observables from discrete first principles while explicitly marking open targets, including coefficient-level source laws for orbital, lensing, and cosmological observables.

## Keywords
quantum gravity; discrete cosmology; black hole entropy; causal structure; spacetime emergence; causal severance; horizon thermodynamics

# Paper IV

## Title
Relational Actualism IV: Complexity, Life, and Recursive Closure in Discrete Causal Networks

## Optional subtitle
A causal-graph framework for biological organization, agency, and emergence

## Resource type
Publication → Preprint

## Description
Relational Actualism IV extends the framework to complexity, life, and cognition.

Using the same discrete causal model developed in the previous papers, the paper investigates the conditions under which organized systems can sustain persistent boundary conditions and exhibit recursive closure. It introduces the Causal Firewall: a set of structural conditions for stable, self-maintaining organization based on redundant boundary inscription and bounded boundary turnover.

Rather than treating biological or cognitive organization as arising from a specific chemical or informational substrate, RA proposes substrate-independent conditions under which stable, life-like organization can emerge in causal networks. The paper connects causal-network structure to biological organization, origin-of-life constraints, biosignature criteria, agency, and consciousness, while identifying open problems in persistence-window calculations, prebiotic network modeling, and higher-order cognitive organization.

## Keywords
complex systems; origin of life; biological organization; causal networks; information and computation; recursive closure; artificial life
