On the Mutual Implication
of Laws and Phenomena
The standard picture

Physics has long maintained a dichotomy: laws on one side, phenomena on the other. The laws are the fixed background against which phenomena play out. This division feels natural, almost definitional.

Relational Actualism dissolves this dichotomy — not by arguing against it. And it does so while preserving what makes phenomena real: the irreversible unfolding of events in time. The arrow of time and the web of mutual implications are two perspectives on one process.

The single commitment

The LLC is not a constraint that the causal graph satisfies; it is the causal graph, stated as a property. Conservation of energy is not a law that actualization events obey; it is what actualization events are, viewed at the level of bookkeeping. The irreversibility of time is encoded in the framework's most primitive commitment, since actualization — by definition — is directed.

The mathematical evidence

When we mapped the open problems, none required a genuinely new physical input. Each was the same constraint — "when does a process become irreversible?" — asked at a different scale. The answer is always ΔS* ≈ 0.601 nats.

O09 (spacelike commutativity) was amplitude locality applied to two observers. O05 (Unruh detector) was rindler_stationarity applied to a measuring device. O08 (biological timescale) revealed a fixed-point: μ = ΔS* universally, the ℓ, Γ, and τ_d all cancelling.

The presentation problem

Human language is linear. But the structure of RA is not a chain — it is a web. The interactive graph to the left is not a diagram about the theory. It is the theory's logical structure, made visible.

The reader can navigate in any order, following edges in either direction. The structure is the same regardless of where they start — because the structure is not imposed on the content, but is the content.

"The laws are not above the phenomena; they are the phenomena, described at the level of pattern rather than instance."
